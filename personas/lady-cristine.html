Lady Cristine Grenewood

Wife of Roger Grenewood, Ventenar

← Back to Personas

Basic Information

Full Name: Lady Cristine Grenewood (née Davenport)

Birth: 26th March 1330, Sible Headingham, Essex

Current Age: 54 years (as of 1384)

Current Status: Wife of Roger Grenewood, Ventenar of Riverbend

Family Background: Daughter of Sir Robert Davenport, knight in service to John de Vere, 7th Earl of Oxford

Previous Marriage: Widowed from Sir Hugh de Clare (killed at Crécy, 1346)

Children: Three sons, all serving as men-at-arms for the English Crown

Timeline

1330 - 1344 (Early Life)

Born in Sible Headingham, Essex, to Sir Robert Davenport, a knight in service to the Earl of Oxford. Raised in the noble household of the Earl of Oxford.

1344 - 1346 (First Marriage)

Married Sir Hugh de Clare, a minor knight in service to the Earl of Oxford. Accompanied her husband on expedition to France with Edward III in 1345. Widowed when Sir Hugh was killed at the Battle of Crécy (26th August 1346).

Sheltered with friends in Rouen while attempting to arrange passage back to England. Met and fell in love with Roger Grenewood, who had fought at Crécy.

Remained in Aquitaine, Normandy, and Brittany while Roger fought in various campaigns. Blessed with three sons who would follow their father's martial path. Supported Roger's service with Edward of Woodstock and subsequent campaigns.

1376 - 1380 (Later Campaigns)

Rejoined the Free Companies with Roger after Edward of Woodstock's death. Continued to support military operations throughout France and Gascony.

1380 - Present (Riverbend Service)

Returned to England with Roger, who took position as Ventenar of Riverbend. Currently in Europe supporting Roger's leadership of the Archers' Free Company.

Detailed Narrative

Born on the 26th of March, 1330, in the quiet Essex village of Sible Headingham, Lady Cristine's life began in the noble household of the Earl of Oxford. As the daughter of Sir Robert Davenport, a knight in service to John de Vere, the 7th Earl of Oxford, she was raised with the values and expectations of the English nobility.

In 1344, at the age of fourteen, Cristine was married to Sir Hugh de Clare, a minor knight also in service to the Earl of Oxford. The young couple's life together was cut short when they accompanied Edward III's expedition to France in 1345. At the Battle of Crécy on the 26th of August, 1346, Sir Hugh fell in battle, leaving Cristine a widow in a foreign land.

Finding herself stranded in France, Cristine sought shelter with friends in Rouen while attempting to arrange passage back to England. It was during this difficult time that she met Roger Grenewood, a yeoman archer who had fought at Crécy. Their shared experience of the battle and its aftermath brought them together, and they were married at the abbey of Saint-Ouen in October 1348.

The years that followed saw Cristine adapting to a life quite different from her noble upbringing. She traveled with Roger and the Free Companies through Normandy, Gascony, and Aquitaine, learning to navigate the challenges of life on campaign. When Edward III renewed the war with France in 1355, she remained in Aquitaine, Normandy, and Brittany while Roger fought in the grande chevauchée that would lead to the Battle of Poitiers.

During these years, Cristine and Roger were blessed with three sons, all of whom would follow in their father's footsteps as men-at-arms in service to the English crown. Despite the hardships of military life, she maintained her noble bearing and provided a stable presence for her family throughout their travels and campaigns.

After the death of Edward of Woodstock in 1376, Cristine and Roger rejoined the Free Companies, continuing their military service throughout France and Gascony. In 1380, they finally returned to England, where Roger took up his position as Ventenar of Riverbend.

Today, Lady Cristine continues to support her husband's leadership of the Archers' Free Company on campaign in Europe. Her life story, from noble daughter to military wife, reflects the complex and often challenging nature of medieval life, particularly for women caught in the midst of the Hundred Years' War.

Her heraldry, combining the Davenport arms (Argent a chevron between 3 crosses crosslet fitchee Sable) with her first husband's De Clare arms (Or three chevrons Gules), serves as a visual reminder of her noble lineage and the different chapters of her remarkable life.
